# Simon - 2026 Personal Goals

## Goal 1: Team Enablement and Stability

### WHY — Purpose
Simon holds deep expertise in the Project Planning architecture that currently creates a single-point-of-knowledge risk for the team.
Now that Simon and [@Daniel] work together in the same POD, they naturally collaborate on new features — but the real risk lies in **already-implemented areas** where Simon holds undocumented architectural knowledge, historical context, and edge-case understanding that [@Daniel] won't encounter through day-to-day POD work alone.
Structured knowledge transfer of these existing ownership areas creates a second technical anchor, reduces bus factor, and enables [@Daniel] to independently maintain and extend critical parts of the codebase.

### S — Specific
Develop [@Daniel] into a credible co-owner for existing, already-implemented architecture in Project Planning by:
1. Identifying 3-5 major technical areas you currently own in Project Planning
2. Selecting 1 area that is **already implemented** and **sufficiently large/complex/important** to create meaningful impact — the focus is on transferring deep knowledge of existing architecture, not co-designing new features (which already happens naturally in the POD)
3. Running structured knowledge transfer sessions: code walkthroughs, architecture decision history, edge cases, debugging strategies, and undocumented conventions
4. Establishing true shared ownership where [@Daniel] can independently debug, extend, and make technical decisions in that area when Simon is unavailable

### M — Measurable
- **By May**: Document 3-5 major technical areas Simon owns in Project Planning and assess their importance/complexity
- **By end Q2**: Select 1 major **already-implemented** technical area based on:
  - Strategic importance to the squad (e.g., `cf-gantt-lib` internals, dependencies-calculation logic, Bryntum integration model, ...)
  - Complexity sufficient to demonstrate senior ownership (non-trivial, cross-cutting concerns — not small isolated features)
  - Knowledge gap: areas where [@Daniel] would struggle without Simon's context, even though they work in the same POD
- **By end Q2**: Begin structured knowledge transfer sessions (architecture walkthroughs, decision history, edge cases) — at least 2 dedicated sessions per month
- **By end Q3**: Complete handover where [@Daniel] independently leads technical approach and decisions in that area
- [@Daniel] (**if required**) presents technical decisions in shaping/grooming sessions and can defend the approach with minimal input from Simon by Q4.
- [@Daniel] (**if required/possible**) represents Simon in cross-team technical discussions

### A — Achievable
Simon is already recognized as the technical anchor for Project Planning with deep domain knowledge — the PP deep dive confirms the breadth of what he owns. Working in the same POD gives [@Daniel] daily access for follow-up questions, making structured knowledge transfer sessions more effective. The handover will occur at a defined point (end Q3), with Simon remaining available for questions afterward. This creates real shared ownership where both can contribute equally to decisions rather than a hierarchical approval structure.

### R — Relevant
This ensures the squad's technical stability in Project Planning scales beyond Simon and frees him to contribute more at the cross-team level. It directly addresses the feedback recommendation to move from "expert" to "enabler." Note: co-designing new features already happens naturally through POD collaboration — this goal deliberately targets the harder problem of transferring ownership of **existing, deeply embedded knowledge** that wouldn't transfer organically.

### T — Time-bound
- End April: Identify and assess technical ownership areas
- Q2: Select target area and begin structured knowledge transfer sessions (at least 2/month)
- Q3: Complete handover with defined transition point (validated by independent contribution)
- Q4: Tbd. Actively bringing the topic into discussions with product and raise concerns when needed in shaping/grooming

## Goal 2: OKR Ownership & Management Visibility

### WHY — Purpose
Simon's deep technical expertise is well recognized within the squad but his impact remains largely scoped to Project Planning. To grow as a senior engineer and gain visibility at the management level, Simon needs to define and drive OKRs that deliver value beyond his immediate app — contributing outcomes that matter to the broader engineering department. Owning cross-cutting OKRs demonstrates strategic thinking, broadens his influence, and makes his contributions legible to leadership.

### S — Specific
Define, own, and drive OKRs that deliver impact at the **engineering department level** to increase visibility toward management by:
1. Defining at least 1 OKR per half-year that addresses a need or initiative relevant to the engineering department as a whole (e.g., cross-app architecture improvements, developer experience, shared tooling, quality initiatives, process improvements)
2. Actively tracking progress with measurable key results and providing regular status updates
3. Presenting OKR outcomes directly to management at least once per half-year — demonstrating cross-team value, not just squad-level delivery (e.g. OKR Review)

### M — Measurable
- **2 OKRs defined** with clear, measurable key results — at least 1 must have scope beyond Project Planning (engineering-wide impact)
- **Regular progress tracking**: Key results updated at least bi-weekly; status visible in team OKR tooling or reports
- **2 management-facing presentations** (1 per half-year): OKR progress or outcomes presented in a OKR review or equivalent forum
- **2 OKRs** completed with documented outcomes by end of Q4

### A — Achievable
Simon already drives significant technical outcomes in Project Planning — this goal challenges him to channel that capability into broader, department-level impact. OKR processes are already established in the organization, so Simon can leverage existing cadences. The co-definition approach (with Marcel's support) ensures alignment with department priorities while giving Simon ownership of the narrative. Simon's architectural knowledge (e.g., Bryntum integration, modularization experience) is transferable to cross-team topics.

### R — Relevant
Moves Simon from squad-scoped impact to department-level visibility — a key step for senior engineers seeking broader recognition. Positions Simon as someone who thinks strategically about engineering outcomes beyond his own app, which is a differentiator at the senior level. Directly supports the team goal of making the squad's contributions visible and valuable across the organization.

### T — Time-bound
- **H1**: Define and drive an OKR across teams; present results in the management-facing meeting (e.g. OKR Review)
- **Between H1 and H2**: Retrospect on H1 OKR and apply lessons learned to the H2 OKR
- **H2**: Define and drive an OKR across teams; present results in the management-facing meeting (e.g. OKR Review)
